kriyA-anaBihiwa_subject I want him to go . This relation connects verbs with nouns or pronouns whose case is marked with some other verb to which they become an object and at the same time they act as a karWA/agent/doer of the other verb. In our example, 'him' is assigned accusative case by the verb 'want' and it acts as a karWA of the verb 'go'. Stanford parser gives "nsubj" relation between 'go' and 'him' but in such sentences the subject 'him' in this example, is not aBihiwa by the kriyA 'go', hence we have given it the name 'anaBihiwa_subject'. nsubj + aux + the word to
kriyA-prayojya_karwA Kaikeyi made Rama do his job . The object of the first verb (i.e. causative verb) becomes prayojya_karwA according to Sanskrit grammar, so we have given this object a prayojya_karwA relation but we are also showing the prayojya_karwA as an object of the causative verb. e.g. "Kaikeyi made RAMA DO his job." here 'RAMA' is prayojya_karwA and is the object of the verb 'made'. ccomp + nsubj + causative verb
kriyA-aBihiwa There are flowers in the garden . The pradicate in this example (flowers) is the subject (aBihiwa_karwA) of the verb. The verb has gender, number and person agreement with it, that is why we also named it kriyA-GNP_agr_subject . (GNP_agr = Gender Number person agreement) but later changed it to kriyA-aBihiwa expl + nsubj

kriyA-preraka_kriyA Kaikeyi made Rama do his job . In English, causative verbs (make, have, get etc.) loose their own meaning and add an extra meaning "to convince to do something" or "to trick someone into doing something" to the other verbs, but in Hindi and other Indian languages causativisation is marked through suffixes, hence we combine the causative verbs with the main verb and mark the causative verbs as preraka_kriyAs. ccomp + causative verb
